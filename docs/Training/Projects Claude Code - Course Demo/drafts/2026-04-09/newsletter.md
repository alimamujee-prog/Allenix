## Idea 1: The bottleneck shifted from AI quality to AI management

### Variation A

Last week I had five terminal tabs open. One building a landing page. One doing competitor research. One drafting content. One restructuring a client onboarding flow. One pulling data for a case study.

And I was clicking between them going "which one was doing what again?"

Here's what caught me off guard. The AI agents have gotten properly good. Not as a sales pitch — I mean they genuinely handle full tasks without me hovering. Set one running, walk away for 10 minutes, come back to something usable. That wasn't possible six months ago.

But that improvement created a problem I didn't see coming.

When the output was mediocre, I could only run one session at a time because I had to babysit everything. One tab. One task. Simple. Now that the agents are good enough to trust, I run five in parallel. And suddenly I'm not doing the work — I'm managing the AI that's doing the work.

### The hidden cost

I tracked it for a week. Every tab switch costs about 2-3 minutes of re-orientation. Re-reading the conversation. Figuring out where the agent left off. Getting my head back into that specific context.

At 15-20 switches a day, that's close to an hour gone. Not on productive work. On navigating between productive work.

The irony is hard to miss. I started using AI to free up my time. Now I'm spending that freed-up time managing AI sessions.

### What this actually means

The AI isn't the bottleneck anymore. The management layer is. And I think that's true for most people running multiple agents in parallel — they just haven't put a name to it yet.

The fix isn't a smarter AI. It's a smarter way to manage what the AI is already doing. Something that shows you all your tasks at once, flags what needs your input, and lets you jump in only when it matters.

If you're running more than two AI sessions at a time, track your tab switches for a day. The number will probably surprise you.

### Variation B

Something shifted in the last few months and I don't think most people have noticed.

AI agents got good enough to run on their own. Not flawless. But good enough that you can hand one a task, do something else, and come back to a solid first draft 10 minutes later.

That sounds like a straightforward win. And it is. But it introduced a problem that crept up on me.

### When capability creates chaos

When the agents were average, I ran one at a time. Full attention. One terminal, one task. Now that they're capable, I run five at once. Five tabs. Five different tasks. Five different contexts. And instead of doing my actual work, I'm clicking between windows trying to remember what each agent is doing.

I spent 3-4 months [VERIFY: confirm exact timeline] running my business this way. Multiple agents in parallel, handling content, client work, system builds — all through terminal windows. The single biggest drag on my productivity wasn't the AI. It was the tab-switching.

### The maths

Every context switch costs something. Not just the seconds to click to another tab — the 2-3 minutes to re-read, re-orient, and get back into what that agent was doing. Stack those up across a day and you're losing close to an hour on navigation alone.

Nobody talks about this because the story is still "AI saves time." And it does. But there's a hidden cost baked into that saved time, and it's the management overhead of running multiple sessions without a system for tracking them.

The terminal was perfectly fine for one session at a time. But agents have outgrown the terminal. What comes next needs to sit above it — showing you all your tasks at once, flagging what needs your input, and letting you manage goals instead of chat windows.

## Idea 2: Every agent management tool is built for developers, not business owners

### Variation A

I went on a proper hunt last month. Tried every tool I could find for managing multiple Claude Code sessions.

Here's the short version: none of them work for business owners.

**Tmux** splits your terminal into panes. You can see several sessions side by side. Useful if you live in the terminal. But you still can't see the big picture. No dragging tasks around. No progress at a glance. Just multiple chat windows scrolling past.

**The Anthropic desktop app** is genuinely nice. Clean UI. Proper chat interface. But still one conversation at a time. Nicer window, same limitation.

**Vibe Kanban** was the one I had high hopes for. A kanban board that spins up Claude sessions automatically. The UI is great. But it's oriented around GitHub commits, pull requests, and branches. If you're a software engineer, brilliant. If you want to say "get this goal done" — too much plumbing showing.

**Paperclip** goes the other direction. An "operating system for running an autonomous company." Org charts, CEO and CTO roles, budgets. I don't need an org chart to write a LinkedIn post and build a landing page.

### The pattern

Every tool starts from the same place: the terminal session. The code. Then they bolt project management on top. It's bottom-up.

But business owners need top-down. Start with the goal. "I want a lead generation system." Let the AI figure out what sessions to spin up, how much planning it needs, and how to get it done.

That's the gap. And it's wide open because nobody's building for the person who wants to delegate a goal, not manage a coding session.

### Variation B

Here's a quick review of every AI agent management tool I tried, and why none of them fit.

**Tmux**: Multiple terminal panes. Still the terminal. No big-picture view.

**Desktop app**: Clean UI, one conversation at a time. Same tab-switching problem in a nicer wrapper.

**Vibe Kanban**: Kanban board that auto-spins Claude sessions. Speaks in commits and pull requests. Developer tool wearing project management clothing.

**Paperclip**: Autonomous company OS. Org charts and role budgets. Interesting concept, massive overkill for most real work.

**Everything else** (Claude Code Board, Mission Control, task viewers): Polished, but all oriented around coding sessions and developer workflows.

### The blind spot

They all answer the same question: how do I manage multiple coding sessions?

Business owners are asking a different one: how do I manage goals and let the AI handle the rest?

Same technology, completely different need. Every tool starts from the terminal and tries to add management on top. What's actually needed is the reverse — start from the goal, let the system handle sessions underneath.

And none of them carry business context. No brand voice. No client details. No memory of previous work. Every session starts from scratch, disconnected from everything that makes your business yours.

That's not a feature gap. That's a category gap.

## Idea 3: Manage goals, not terminals

### Variation A

I want to tell you about the worst hire I never made.

For the last few months, I've been managing AI agents the way a bad manager manages people. Hovering. Checking in every few minutes. Switching between tasks obsessively. Never actually delegating.

Not because the agents couldn't handle it. They can. Claude Code runs full tasks autonomously now. Planning, research, building, iterating — all without me watching.

But the tools I was using forced me into micromanagement. One terminal per task. One chat window per agent. The only way to know what was happening was to click in and read the conversation.

### What delegation actually looks like

When you hire someone good, you don't watch them type. You give them a goal. A deadline. You say "figure it out and update me when there's progress."

That's the level of abstraction AI tools need to reach. Not "here's a terminal where you can chat with an agent." But "here's where you describe what you want done. The system handles the rest."

"Build me a content repurposing system." That should be the entire input from your side. The AI decides how much planning is needed. How many sub-tasks to create. What skills to use. You only get pulled in when it needs a decision.

### The missing layer

Every tool out there manages sessions. What's missing is something that manages goals. Start from the top — what do you want to achieve? — and let everything underneath sort itself out.

The agents are ready for this. They can handle the delegation. It's the management layer between you and them that hasn't caught up.

What business goal would you hand off to an AI today if the right interface existed?

### Variation B

Here's how most people use Claude Code right now:

Open a terminal. Start a task. Wait 5-10 minutes for the agent to work. Get restless. Open another terminal. Start another task. Repeat until you've got five tabs and no idea what's happening in any of them.

Now here's how it should work:

"Build me a lead generation system."

That's it. That's your entire input.

### What happens underneath

The system figures out what sessions to spin up. How much planning the task needs. How many agents to run in parallel. What skills and context to load.

You don't see terminals. You don't see chat logs. You see goals. Progress. And a flag when the AI needs your input on something.

### Why this matters

Every tool I've found works bottom-up. Start with the terminal, the code session, the chat window. Then try to add project management on top.

Business owners need the opposite direction. Top-down. Start with the goal. Let the technical layer handle itself.

It's the same principle behind every good hire. You manage the outcome, not the process. You don't care how many emails your employee sends or what order they tackle their sub-tasks. You care whether the goal gets hit.

The agents are capable of handling that level of trust right now. The management tools just haven't caught up.

Track how much of your day goes to managing AI sessions versus reviewing finished output. That ratio tells you everything about where the bottleneck actually is.

## Idea 4: Traditional kanban breaks with AI agents

### Variation A

I tried to manage my AI agents with a standard kanban board last month. Four columns. Not started. In progress. Review. Done. Cards flowing left to right.

It fell apart within a day.

### Why it breaks

Kanban assumes work flows in one direction. A task starts, gets worked on, gets reviewed, gets completed. That's how human workflows tend to move. Hand something to a colleague, they do it, they hand it back, you approve it. Maybe one revision. Done.

AI agent work doesn't move like that. It bounces.

Here's what actually happens: I give Claude a task. Claude starts working. Claude comes back with a clarifying question. I answer. Claude goes back to work. Claude produces a draft. I give feedback. Claude revises. I review again. Claude makes one more adjustment. Done.

That's five or six transitions between "my turn" and "Claude's turn" for a single task. On a kanban board, I'd be dragging the same card from "in progress" to "review" and back again repeatedly. That's not project management. That's busy work dressed up as organisation.

### What works instead

Two states. Your turn and Claude's turn.

On one side, everything that needs your attention right now. On the other, everything Claude is actively working on. Click into a task, see the last exchange, give your input, push it back.

It maps to how agent collaboration actually works — iterative, back-and-forth, with clear handoff points — instead of forcing it into a linear framework built for human teams passing work down a line.

Small shift. Massive difference in how manageable five parallel tasks actually feel.

### Variation B

Kanban boards were built on an assumption that nobody questions: work flows in one direction.

Not started becomes in progress. In progress becomes review. Review becomes done. Left to right. Linear. Clean.

That assumption works for human teams. You hand a task to someone, they complete it, they hand it back. Maybe one round of feedback. The card moves right and stays there.

### AI agents don't work that way

When I give Claude a task, the work oscillates. Claude works, then asks me something. I respond, Claude works again. Claude shows me output, I give feedback. Claude revises, I review. Back and forth, sometimes five or six rounds before a single task is finished.

I tried tracking this on a regular kanban board. Within an hour I was dragging the same card between columns so often it felt absurd. The board wasn't helping me manage anything. It was just creating clicks.

### The simpler model

Strip it down to two states: your turn and Claude's turn.

When something needs my input — a question, a draft to review, a decision — it sits on my side. When Claude is working, it sits on Claude's side. I look at my side, handle whatever's there, push things back.

No columns for "blocked" or "QA" or "ready for deploy." Just: who has the ball right now?

It sounds almost too simple. But when you're running five or six tasks in parallel with AI agents, simple is the only thing that actually scales. Anything more and you end up managing the board instead of managing the work.

The tools built for human teams need rethinking for AI collaboration. Not a complete overhaul. Just an honest look at how the work actually moves.
