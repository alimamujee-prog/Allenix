## Idea 1: The bottleneck shifted from AI quality to AI management

### Variation A

1/ Six months ago the problem with AI agents was obvious. The output wasn't good enough. You had to babysit everything.

2/ That problem is gone. Claude Code agents handle full tasks autonomously now. Set one running, walk away, come back to a solid result.

3/ But it created a new problem. When the AI was mediocre, I ran one session at a time. Now I run five in parallel. And I spend more time switching between tabs than reviewing output.

4/ Every tab switch costs 2-3 minutes of re-reading. At 15+ switches a day, that's close to an hour lost on navigation alone.

5/ The AI isn't the bottleneck anymore. The way you're managing it is.

### Variation B

1/ Five terminal tabs. Five AI agents running different tasks. Me clicking between them going "which one was doing my research again?"

2/ That's my last few months. Claude Code got good enough to trust, so I started running more sessions in parallel.

3/ The irony: I started using AI to free up time. Now I'm spending that time managing the AI.

4/ The tool improved. The workflow didn't. And the workflow is what's actually costing you now.

## Idea 2: Every agent management tool is built for developers, not business owners

### Variation A

1/ Tested every Claude Code management tool out there. Tmux, Vibe Kanban, Paperclip, the desktop app, Mission Control.

2/ They all answer the same question: how do I manage multiple coding sessions? GitHub commits. Pull requests. Branches.

3/ Business owners have a different question: how do I manage goals and let the AI handle the technical bits?

4/ Every tool starts from the terminal and bolts management on top. What's needed is the reverse — start from the goal, let the system figure out the sessions.

5/ Nobody's building for the business user who just wants to say "get this done." The gap is wide open.

### Variation B

1/ Tried Tmux. Still a terminal. Tried the desktop app. Still one conversation at a time. Tried Vibe Kanban. Built for developers. Tried Paperclip. An org chart to write a LinkedIn post.

2/ Every tool assumes you care about code diffs and pull requests. Most business owners don't know what those are.

3/ The question developers ask: how do I manage coding sessions? The question business owners ask: how do I manage goals?

4/ Same technology. Completely different need. Nobody's serving the second one yet.

## Idea 3: Manage goals, not terminals

### Variation A

1/ You don't hire someone and then watch them type every email. You give them a goal. "Here's the deadline. Figure it out. Update me when there's progress."

2/ So why am I still staring at terminal windows watching AI agents work character by character?

3/ The shift: stop managing sessions, start managing goals. "Build me a lead gen system." That's the input. Planning, sub-tasks, sessions — all handled underneath.

4/ The agents are capable of this level of delegation right now. It's the management layer that hasn't caught up.

### Variation B

1/ How most people use Claude Code: open terminal, start task, get bored, open another terminal, repeat until five tabs and zero clarity.

2/ How it should work: describe the goal. The system figures out the planning, the agents, the sub-tasks, the skills needed.

3/ You get pulled in when it needs a decision. Otherwise it just works.

4/ Same principle as a good hire. You manage the outcome, not the process. The agents are ready. The tools aren't.

## Idea 4: Traditional kanban breaks with AI agents

### Variation A

1/ Tried managing AI agents on a kanban board. Not started, in progress, review, done. Lasted about a day.

2/ AI work doesn't flow left to right. It bounces. You give Claude a task. Claude asks a question. You answer. Claude drafts. You give feedback. Claude revises. Five or six handoffs per task.

3/ Dragging a card between "in progress" and "review" six times isn't project management. It's admin theatre.

4/ What works: two columns. Your turn. Claude's turn. One glance tells you everything that needs your attention right now.

### Variation B

1/ Kanban assumes work flows in one direction. Not started to done, left to right. Clean and linear.

2/ AI agent work is the opposite. It's iterative. Back and forth between you and the AI, sometimes five or six times per task.

3/ The frameworks built for human teams don't map to AI collaboration. The work bounces, it doesn't flow.

4/ Simpler model: your turn vs their turn. Handle what's on your side, push it back. That's it.
