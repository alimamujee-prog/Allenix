## Idea 3: Manage goals, not terminals

### Variation A

You don't hire an employee and then stand behind them watching every keystroke.

You give them a goal. A deadline. You say "figure it out and update me when there's progress." That's delegation.

So why are I still staring at terminal windows watching AI agents work?

Claude Code agents can plan, research, build, and iterate without me hovering. But I'm still managing one terminal per task. Still clicking between tabs. Still re-reading conversations to figure out where things stand.

The shift that needs to happen is straightforward. Stop managing sessions. Start managing goals.

"Build me a content repurposing system." That's the input. Everything else — planning, sub-tasks, sessions, skills — gets handled underneath.

The AI decides how much planning it needs. How many sub-tasks to break it into. What tools to use. You only get pulled in when it needs a decision from you.

That's not a future feature. It's buildable right now. The agents are capable of it. It's the management layer that's missing.

What's the first goal you'd hand off completely to an AI agent if you could?

## Idea 4: Traditional kanban breaks with AI agents

### Variation A

I tried managing AI agents with a standard kanban board. Not started. In progress. Review. Done.

It lasted about a day.

Here's what actually happens when you work with an AI agent. You give it a task. It starts working. It comes back with a clarifying question. You answer. It goes back to work. It produces a draft. You give feedback. It revises. You review again. It adjusts.

That's five or six handoffs for a single task. On a kanban board, I'd be dragging the same card from "in progress" to "review" and back again repeatedly.

That's not project management. That's admin theatre.

What actually works is simpler. Two states: your turn and Claude's turn.

Everything that needs your attention sits on one side. Everything the AI is actively working on sits on the other. Click into a task, see the last exchange, give your input, push it back.

It maps to how agent collaboration actually works. Iterative. Back and forth. Clear handoff points. Not a linear assembly line built for human teams.

How are you tracking what needs your attention versus what the AI is still working on?

### Variation B

Kanban boards have a hidden assumption built in: work flows in one direction.

Not started becomes in progress. In progress becomes review. Review becomes done. Left to right. Linear. Clean.

That assumption falls apart the moment you start working with AI agents.

When I give Claude a task, the work doesn't flow. It bounces. Claude works, then asks me something. I respond, Claude works again. Claude shows me a draft, I give feedback. Claude revises, I review. Back and forth, sometimes five or six times before a single task is finished.

Dragging the same card between columns six times isn't managing anything. It's just creating clicks.

So I stripped it back to what actually matters. Two columns. Your turn. Claude's turn.

At a glance I see everything waiting for my input on one side. Everything Claude is actively working on the other. Handle what's there, push things back. That's the full interaction.

When you've got five tasks running in parallel with AI agents, simple is the only thing that scales.

What framework are you using to manage your AI workflows right now?
