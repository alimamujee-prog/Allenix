# Prompt 3: Medical Spa Pain Points, Quick Wins & LTV Levers — Ranked by Impact

Research the specific operational pain points in medical spas and aesthetic practices, then rank every automation and process improvement opportunity by effort vs impact. Weight the ranking toward patient lifetime value (retention, rebooking, expansion) — not just acquisition.

## 1. Operational Pain Points — The Real Stuff
Research the actual day-to-day problems that cost med spa owners time and money:
- No-show and cancellation rates — real data, not estimates
- How much time front desk spends on phone/email/text per day
- Patient follow-up and rebooking — what falls through the cracks
- Payment collection problems (failed payments, outstanding balances, payment plans)
- Inventory waste (expired products, stockouts, cash tied up)
- Staff onboarding and training burden on the owner
- Documentation and compliance overhead
- Reporting blind spots — what they can't see but wish they could

For each pain point, find:
- Specific data (hours lost, dollars lost, frequency)
- Real quotes from practice owners if available
- Whether they've tried to solve it and what happened

## 2. Patient Retention & LTV Levers (Prioritize This Section)
Most med spas focus on getting new patients. Research the right side of the bowtie — retention and lifetime value:
- **Typical patient retention rates** — what % return after first visit? After 90 days? After 1 year?
- **Rebooking rates** — how often is the next appointment booked before the patient leaves?
- **Average patient lifetime value** — total spend over the full relationship
- **Revenue impact of retention improvements** — what does a 10% improvement in retention do to the bottom line?
- **Why patients don't come back** — the specific reasons and where in the lifecycle they drop off
- **Treatment adherence** — are patients completing their recommended treatment plans?
- **Win-back campaigns** — what works to recover lapsed patients?

## 3. Acquisition Pain Points (Still Important)
- Lead response times — how fast do practices respond, and how fast should they?
- Consultation no-show rates and what causes them
- Website and booking funnel abandonment
- Cost per new patient by channel (Google Ads, social, referrals, organic)
- What marketing spend is wasted or untrackable

## 4. Ranked Opportunity Matrix
Rank ALL automation and process improvement opportunities using this framework:

For each opportunity, provide:
- **Effort Score** (1-10, where 1 = can implement in days with minimal disruption)
- **Impact Score** (1-10, where 10 = transformative revenue or time savings)
- **Time to ROI** (days/weeks)
- **Monthly Cost** (software/tools needed)
- **LTV Impact** (does this improve patient lifetime value, or just acquisition?)
- **Risk Level** (what could go wrong)

**Rank them in three tiers:**

### Tier 1: Retention & LTV Wins (right side of bowtie — PRIORITIZE)
- Automated rebooking sequences
- Post-treatment follow-up and care check-ins
- Treatment adherence tracking and nudges
- Win-back campaigns for lapsed patients
- Patient lifecycle communication automation
- Loyalty and membership program automation
- Proactive churn detection

### Tier 2: Revenue Recovery Wins
- No-show prevention sequences
- Abandoned booking recovery
- Failed payment retry and recovery
- Waitlist and cancellation fill automation
- Deposit and cancellation policy enforcement

### Tier 3: Acquisition & Efficiency Wins
- AI phone receptionist for overflow calls
- Automated review generation
- Instant lead response
- Smart scheduling optimization
- Digital intake and forms automation
- Staff training and onboarding systems

## 5. Real Case Studies
Find actual examples of practices that implemented these improvements. For each:
- Practice size and type
- What they implemented
- Specific before/after metrics (dollars, percentages, time)
- How long implementation took
- What went wrong or surprised them

## Research Focus
Prioritize REAL data over estimates. Look for:
- Practice management platform data (Zenoti, Mindbody, PatientNow benchmarks)
- Industry surveys with specific metrics
- Vendor case studies with verified results
- Practice owner interviews and testimonials
- Academic or industry research on patient retention in aesthetic medicine
- Healthcare retention benchmarks for comparison

## Output Format
1. **Pain point summary** with real data and owner quotes
2. **Patient LTV analysis** — what the data says about retention rates, churn points, and LTV opportunity
3. **Ranked opportunity matrix** (all three tiers in one table, sorted by Impact/Effort ratio)
4. **Top 5 detailed breakdowns** — implementation steps, expected results, tools needed, pitfalls
5. **The retention math** — model showing what a 10%, 20%, and 30% improvement in retention does to annual revenue for a typical $2M-$5M practice

## Constraints
Focus on solutions that:
- Can be implemented in under 30 days for quick wins
- Require less than $500/month in tools for the practice
- Don't require the owner to do significant work
- Work with common practice management platforms (Zenoti, Mindbody, PatientNow)
- Are HIPAA-compliant for patient-facing automation

Optimizing Medical Aesthetics: Operational Pain Points, Quick Wins, and Lifetime Value LeversThe medical aesthetics sector is currently navigating a period of unprecedented expansion, with industry valuations surging past $17 billion and exhibiting an annual growth rate exceeding $1 billion. By 2031, the global medical spa market is projected to reach $47.17 billion. Concurrently, the total number of medical spas in the United States has increased to 10,488, generating an average annual revenue of $1,398,833 per location. Top-tier, membership-focused practices are pushing these boundaries further, routinely exceeding $2.19 million to $2.87 million in annual per-location revenue.However, beneath this top-line hyper-growth lies a complex operational reality. As the market matures, practice owners face increasing macroeconomic pressures, rising supply costs, and aggressive market consolidation. Top-line revenue does not seamlessly translate to bottom-line profitability. Instead, profit margins—which average between 20% and 25%—are frequently eroded by operational inefficiencies, hidden administrative burdens, and a systemic failure to maximize patient lifetime value (LTV). A critical pivot is required: shifting the strategic focus away from relentless, high-cost patient acquisition toward rigorous patient retention, workflow automation, and margin preservation.1. Operational Pain Points — The Invisible Margin LeaksThe most significant threats to a medical spa's profitability are rarely found within the treatment room. They are embedded in administrative workflows, front-desk triage, compliance protocols, and inventory management. These operational bottlenecks consume thousands of hours of staff time, diminish the patient experience, and create invisible but catastrophic margin leaks.No-Show and Cancellation RatesMissed appointments represent a dual financial penalty for aesthetic practices. First, the practice forfeits the anticipated revenue of the scheduled treatment. Second, it absorbs the fixed overhead costs of idle provider time and unutilized capital equipment. Nationally, healthcare no-show rates hover between 23% and 34%, costing the broader U.S. healthcare system an estimated $150 billion annually.In the medical aesthetics sector, missed appointments cost individual practices up to $215,000 annually, with each single no-show representing an average of $200 in direct lost revenue. The secondary costs are equally damaging to operational flow. When a treatment room equipped with a $150,000 to $240,000 energy-based device (such as a CO2 laser) sits empty, the practice's return on invested capital degrades rapidly. Furthermore, since 2021, nearly 49% of medical groups have reported an aggressive rise in patient no-show rates.Despite the severity of this pain point, the solution is largely mechanical. Data indicates that medical spas implementing consistent, automated SMS and email reminders observe their no-show rates plummet to between 8% and 12%, compared to the 17% to 22% rates common in practices lacking automated follow-up.Front Desk Overhead and Communication Blind SpotsThe front desk serves as the operational nucleus of a medical spa, yet it is frequently under-resourced, technologically constrained, and highly susceptible to burnout. Industry research reveals that front desk staff at medical aesthetics practices spend up to 40% of their daily time on repetitive, low-value administrative tasks such as answering routine operational questions, manually collecting patient intake information, and routing phone calls.The volume of inbound communication is staggering. Primary care and specialty medical practices receive an average of 53 inbound calls per physician per day, and specialized aesthetic practices experience similar volumes due to the highly consultative nature of the services. Despite the proliferation of digital booking tools, 67% of patients still prefer calling the practice directly, and an overwhelming 78.11% of med spa appointments are ultimately scheduled via phone, in-person interactions, or manual recurring systems.Because of this heavy reliance on manual phone management, up to 37% of calls to salons and spas go unanswered during peak operational hours. Every missed call is a missed acquisition or retention opportunity. The toll on the staff is immense; 91% of U.S. workers experience burnout, and in healthcare, the administrative burden is a primary driver. When automated solutions are applied to these communication blind spots, the relief is palpable. As one practice manager noted after implementing virtual and AI-assisted receptionist support, the front desk staff could finally "complete a full sentence with the person standing in front of them".Payment Collection and Membership FrictionThe transition toward membership models is recognized as one of the most reliable growth strategies in modern aesthetics. Membership-based spas—defined as those deriving at least 30% of their revenue from recurring subscriptions—demonstrate higher revenue consistency, greater resilience during economic downturns, and significantly faster scale. In 2023, package sales at membership-based spas grew by 85%, adding over $20,000 in incremental revenue per top-performing location.However, managing these subscriptions introduces a severe operational pain point: involuntary churn. Involuntary churn, resulting from failed recurring payments, accounts for 20% to 40% of all subscription cancellations. Generic point-of-sale (POS) systems frequently lack intelligent dunning capabilities (automated failed payment recovery). When a patient's credit card expires, declines due to insufficient funds, or triggers a processor error, front desk staff must manually intervene. This creates an awkward, high-friction patient interaction and consumes valuable administrative time.Furthermore, aesthetic practices face unique vulnerabilities regarding chargebacks. In an industry where service outcomes are often subjective, client disputes are elevated. Generic payment processors lack the specialized reporting required to link time-stamped treatment records and consent forms to payment logs, resulting in lost disputes, dispute fees, and debilitating reserve holds placed on the practice's daily cash flow. Additionally, while offering patient financing options (like CareCredit or Cherry) increases the average transaction value, 41% of practice owners cite high merchant fees as a major hurdle that actively eats into treatment profitability.Inventory Waste and Capital Lock-UpUnlike traditional retail environments, medical spa inventory involves highly sensitive, temperature-controlled, and strictly expiration-dated medical products. Poor inventory management forces medical spas to lose 10% to 15% of their potential revenue. Across the broader healthcare supply chain, product expiration accounts for an average of 8% to 10% of total supply spend.For a medical spa owner, discovering expired neurotoxins, dermal fillers, or specialized chemical peels translates to immediate, unrecoverable capital destruction—often hundreds of dollars per vial. Overstocking ties up critical operating cash flow in products that may expire before use, while stockouts result in canceled appointments, wasted provider time, and damaged patient trust. Practices utilizing manual tracking methods, such as spreadsheets or periodic physical counts, suffer from inconsistent documentation, delayed identification of discrepancies, and an inability to accurately tie product usage to specific treatments or providers. Conversely, practices implementing systematic, automated inventory management reduce waste from expired products by 40% to 60% while maintaining optimal in-stock rates.Staff Onboarding and Training BurdenAesthetic medicine is a high-skill, high-liability industry, making the recruitment and onboarding of qualified staff a massive financial and temporal burden. The direct cost of onboarding a new employee ranges from $4,000 to $7,000 for administrative roles, and can soar between $7,500 and $28,000 for specialized clinical providers. Turnover exacerbates this issue; replacing an employee costs between 100% and 300% of their salary.For practice owners, the burden extends beyond direct capital expenditure. The ramp-up time for a new clinical provider can take one to three months for general roles, and up to eight months for complex enterprise or specialized injection roles. During this period, the owner or medical director must divert time from revenue-generating clinical activities to supervise, shadow, and train. Manual onboarding processes, lacking centralized standard operating procedures (SOPs) or automated learning management systems, force owners and HR managers to repeatedly answer the same procedural questions, consuming 20% to 30% of their operational capacity.Documentation and Compliance OverheadThe regulatory landscape for medical spas is tightening aggressively, introducing severe compliance overhead. A 2025 joint investigation in New York City resulted in 223 inspections of medical spas, uncovering rampant violations including expired and counterfeit products, unauthorized medical procedures, dirty needles, and the presence of controlled substances like Fentanyl and Propofol without proper oversight.The legal and compliance risks of operating without strict documentation protocols are existential. Dr. Kennedy, an industry expert, warned: "When complications occur in an environment with poor oversight, the damage is often not something we can simply reverse. Patients are left with physical scars, emotional trauma, and in some cases permanent disfigurement". Speaker Adrienne Adams echoed this sentiment regarding operational integrity: "Every New Yorker must be able to trust that city businesses they visit comply with state legal regulations to protect consumers' health and safety".To remain compliant, med spas must maintain exhaustive documentation, including HIPAA-compliant charting, consent forms, and lot tracking for all injectables. When these processes are handled manually or via paper clipboards, staff time is wasted, and critical audit trails are lost. Health systems that automate clinical forms save thousands of hours; for example, Montage Health saved 537 hours for registration staff over just six weeks by digitizing patient intake.Reporting Blind SpotsThe reliance on fragmented, disconnected software systems creates severe "attribution blindness" for medical spa owners. For example, a patient researching neurotoxins, laser treatments, and body contouring across multiple digital channels may appear in a practice's CRM as three separate, low-quality leads. In reality, this is a single, highly qualified patient with an $8,000 to $15,000 lifetime value potential.Without centralized business intelligence, owners resort to tracking vanity metrics rather than core KPIs. They cannot accurately visualize their consult-to-conversion rate, their true cost per acquisition (CPA), or provider utilization rates. As Zenoti CEO Sudheer Koneru emphasizes, "Benchmarks help every business owner compare to peers, identify high-impact strategies, and set achievable goals". Courtney Anderson, founder of Spa Trouvé, summarized the pain point perfectly: "I'm data obsessed and I think it's been a key to our success. It's essential to know which areas of your business are growing, which are down, and how others in the industry are performing". Without this data, decisions are made on intuition rather than empirical fact.2. Patient Retention and LTV Levers (Priority Focus)The medical aesthetics industry suffers from a systemic over-indexing on patient acquisition at the direct expense of patient retention. Economic models definitively prove that retaining an existing patient is far more cost-effective than acquiring a new one—costing approximately five times less. More importantly, repeat clients spend 67% more than first-time visitors, and a mere 5% increase in client retention can boost practice profits by 25% to 95%.Retention Benchmarks and The LTV OpportunityThe aesthetic patient base inherently desires long-term relationships; an average of 73% of medical spa patients are repeat visitors, up from 65% in previous years. However, there is a stark divide in how effectively practices capture this returning intent. The industry average retention rate hovers around 50%, while top-performing clinics systematically maintain retention rates of 60% to 80%.Similarly, rebooking rates—defined as a patient booking their next appointment before leaving the clinic—vary drastically based on operational maturity. The industry average for rebooking within 24 hours of an appointment is 39%, but top-earning practices achieve an exceptional 74% rebooking rate.The financial implications of these metrics are profound. The average consumer spends between $450 and $700 per visit, with the national average reported at $527 to $536. Because aesthetic procedures like neuromodulators (which account for 53% of popular services) require maintenance every 3 to 4 months, a loyal client generates between $5,000 and $15,000 in lifetime procedural value (LTV) over the course of a multi-year relationship.Expansion via Retail Cross-SellingLifetime value is further expanded through retail skincare sales. Retail sales of physician-dispensed skincare products account for approximately 20% of total med spa revenue. Across all aesthetic physician segments, 50% of practices report generating over 10% of their total revenue solely from skincare retailing. Patients are currently seeking value by simplifying at-home regimens and consolidating around trusted clinical brands like SkinCeuticals and EltaMD. By integrating retail cross-selling into the post-treatment protocol, practices increase the Average Revenue Per Visit (ARPV) without consuming additional clinical time.Why Patients Do Not ReturnPatients rarely leave a medical spa due to catastrophic clinical failures; they leave due to operational neglect, communication breakdowns, and perceived friction. Research into patient absenteeism and churn highlights several core drivers:The Post-Treatment Silence: The most expensive gap in aesthetic operations is the failure to follow up. When a patient spends $3,000 on a Morpheus8 package and receives no follow-up communication within 48 hours, the relationship feels entirely transactional, and they feel like a "dollar sign rather than a patient".Emotional and Logistical Barriers: In surveys regarding missed healthcare appointments, 65% of patients cited emotional barriers, and 44% noted a perceived disrespect of their time by the health system. Logistical issues such as transportation, childcare, and a lack of understanding of the scheduling system also contribute heavily to drop-off.Lack of Journey-Based Planning: Many spas sell individual sessions rather than journey-based treatment series. This forces the patient to make a brand-new purchasing decision at every single visit, inducing decision fatigue and increasing the likelihood of churn, especially if early results are subtle.Treatment Adherence and Series CompletionCertain aesthetic treatments require rigid adherence to clinical timelines to yield results. For instance, laser hair removal requires between two and six sessions, while body contouring modalities like CoolSculpting or TruSculpt require strict interval spacing. If a practice relies entirely on the patient to remember to book their follow-up, adherence plummets. Baseline medical studies show that while self-reported adherence to topical treatments is 92%, actual measured adherence is often closer to 60.6%. Practices that automate these follow-ups, or utilize aesthetic treatment plan templates linked directly to automated scheduling workflows, see drastically higher series completion rates.Win-Back Campaigns for Lapsed PatientsA dormant patient database is frequently a practice's largest untapped financial asset. One industry case study revealed a practice with 2,000 past patients, 65% of whom had been dormant for over six months. With an average treatment value of $454, this represented $590,200 in theoretically recoverable revenue.Win-back campaigns using SMS are highly effective, boasting a 98% open rate compared to a mere 37% open rate for standard email. The most successful campaigns do not rely on steep discounts, which devalue the brand and train patients to wait for flash sales. Instead, they utilize a value-first approach. For example, a segmented SMS to a lapsed facial client might offer a complimentary consultation to reassess their skin, or provide priority access to a new technology. This reactivates dormant LTV at a fraction of the cost of acquiring a net-new patient.3. Acquisition Pain Points (The Top of the Funnel)While retention drives ultimate profitability, a healthy acquisition funnel is necessary to replace natural demographic attrition and facilitate business scale. The primary pain points in acquisition center on response delays, funnel abandonment, and inefficient capital allocation.Speed-to-Lead and Response TimesPatient interest is at its absolute peak at the exact moment a prospect submits a digital inquiry. When response times lag because front desk staff are occupied with in-clinic patients, leads go cold rapidly. Top-performing practices aim for a response time of under five minutes during operating hours. If a practice pays between $150 and $350 to acquire a single lead, failing to respond promptly wastes capital and surrenders market share to faster, more technologically adept competitors.Consultation No-Shows and Conversion RatesThe average lead-to-booking conversion rate for med spas in the U.S. hovers between 8% and 12%, though high-performing practices push this beyond 20% through rigorous funnel optimization. Once a consultation is successfully booked, the goal is to keep the consultation no-show rate below 15%.The consultation-to-treatment conversion rate is the ultimate metric separating average clinics from market leaders. The industry average sits between 30% and 40%, whereas top-performing spas successfully convert 60% to 70% of consultations into paying treatments. This massive gap is rarely a result of better top-of-funnel marketing; it is the product of superior in-clinic sales processes, value-based education, structured lead qualification, and the strategic use of social proof (before-and-after photos, verified reviews).Website and Booking Funnel AbandonmentApproximately 21.89% of med spa appointments are now booked online. However, many potential patients abandon the booking process halfway through due to friction in the software interface, lack of clear pricing, or interface confusion. Implementing automated cart recovery sequences—following up with users who started but did not complete a booking—is a massive revenue lever. Zenoti benchmark data indicates that automated follow-up on abandoned bookings generates approximately $800 in recovered revenue per location per month for businesses that utilize it.Cost Per Acquisition (CPA) and Wasted Marketing SpendMany practices struggle to measure the true return on investment (ROI) of their marketing spend, resulting in untrackable waste. A healthy marketing ROI for a medical spa is generally considered to be 400% to 600% (a 4:1 to 6:1 ratio). The average cost per acquisition (CPA) for a new aesthetic patient is approximately $132.Different channels yield drastically different efficiencies. For instance, the average conversion rate for the health and medical industry on Google Ads is 3.36%, whereas Meta (Facebook/Instagram) Ads for the beauty and cosmetics sector can yield conversion rates of 5.93%. Despite the availability of this data, an astonishing 77% of practices struggle with brand differentiation, and 60% of practices generate less than $500,000 annually, indicating widespread marketing inefficiency and an inability to convert digital impressions into booked revenue.4. Automated Rebooking and Post-Care Sequences (Tier 1: Retention)The Concept: The vast majority of patient drop-off occurs because the practice fails to guide the patient to their next logical step. Implementing an automated SMS system that sends customized post-care instructions 24 to 48 hours after a visit, followed by a personalized rebooking prompt 6 to 8 weeks later, creates an inescapable engagement loop.Implementation Steps: Map the treatment lifecycles for your top 5 revenue-generating services. For example, neurotoxins require follow-ups every 3 to 4 months; specific resurfacing lasers every 4 to 6 weeks. Configure automated SMS triggers in platforms like Zenoti, PatientNow, or via an overlay CRM tool like SurgePoint, to message patients dynamically based on the specific service code billed.Expected Results: Consistent implementation increases 90-day retention rates by 10% to 20%. It reliably recovers patients who genuinely intended to return but forgot due to life logistics.Tools Needed: Existing Practice Management (PM) software (Zenoti/Mindbody) or third-party aesthetic CRM (SurgePoint, Prospyr) ($50-$150/mo).Pitfalls: Sending generic, uninspired "we miss you" emails. Messages must be specific to the treatment received (e.g., "Hi Sarah, it is time to maintain your Dysport results") and delivered via SMS to capitalize on the 98% open rate.2. Automated Review and Referral Generation (Tier 3: Acquisition & Efficiency)The Concept: Local SEO positioning and social proof are the primary drivers of low-CPA patient acquisition. Furthermore, patients referred by friends exhibit higher initial trust, higher LTVs, and lower churn rates. Yet, front desk staff rarely remember to ask for reviews or referrals during chaotic checkout periods.Implementation Steps: Set an automated SMS trigger to fire exactly 24 hours post-treatment. Utilize smart filtering logic: ask the patient to rate their experience via SMS from 1 to 5. Ratings of 4 or 5 instantly receive a direct link to the practice's Google Business Profile and a digital referral code to share with friends. Ratings of 1 to 3 are bypassed from public review sites and routed immediately to a private management inbox for service recovery.Expected Results: Rapid, exponential aggregation of 5-star Google reviews, pushing the clinic to the top of the Google Local Pack for "near me" search results. One med spa (BeVibrant) generated 22 net-new, highly qualified referral patients in a single week using this exact methodology.Tools Needed: SurgePoint, Podium, or native CRM reputation management modules ($100-$300/mo).Pitfalls: Asking for the review too late in the cycle. The 24-hour mark is the peak emotional high of the aesthetic result; waiting a week drastically lowers completion rates.3. Automated Dunning and Failed Payment Recovery (Tier 2: Revenue Recovery)The Concept: Involuntary churn from failed credit cards destroys the predictable revenue foundation of a membership model. Manual follow-up by staff is socially awkward, operationally inefficient, and prone to human error. Automated dunning software systematically retries cards at statistically optimized times and sends polite, self-serve update links to patients.Implementation Steps: Connect a dunning management tool or activate the native dunning features in the POS system. Set a "smart retry" schedule. For example, wait 2 to 3 days before the first retry, strictly avoid weekends and early mornings, and craft empathetic SMS/email notifications with secure, encrypted links for patients to update their card details independently.Expected Results: Recovers 40% to 70% of failed payments without manual staff intervention, instantly protecting recurring monthly revenue (MRR).Tools Needed: Native features in advanced PMs (Zenoti, PatientNow) or specialized subscription overlay tools compatible with the payment processor ($50-$200/mo).Pitfalls: Over-messaging the patient or using aggressive language. "Soft declines" (e.g., temporary processor errors) should be retried silently by the software before alerting the patient of a "hard decline".4. # Allenix Addendum: Marginal Gains Per Bowtie Conversion Point

> WbD's Mathematical Model applied to the med spa vertical. Small improvements at each Bowtie stage compound. You don't need one big fix — you need marginal gains at the right points. Allenix leads with the highest-leverage points first (CR6, CR7, CR8 — the right side of the Bowtie) and tackles the rest in subsequent phases.

## For a typical $5M practice (3,100 active patients, $536/visit, 3 visits/year)

| Bowtie Stage | What It Measures | Current Benchmark | +10% Improvement | Annual Revenue Gain |
|--------------|------------------|-------------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| CR1 (Visit→Inquiry) | Lead-to-inquiry rate | 8% | 8.8% | ~$97K |
| CR4 (Consult→Treatment) | Consult-to-treatment conversion | 35% | 38.5% | ~$97K |
| CR6 (First-visit Rebook) | % rebooking before leaving | 39% | 42.9% | ~$61K |
| CR7 (90-day Retention) | % returning within treatment cycle | 50% | 55% | ~$500K |
| CR8 (Expansion) | % adding services, retail, membership | 15% | 16.5% | ~$54K |

**The takeaway:** CR7 (retention) is the biggest single lever by 5x. That's why Allenix leads with retention. But the full picture shows that once retention is fixed, the next gains come from CR4 (consult conversion) and CR6 (rebooking at checkout) — both right-side-adjacent. Acquisition (CR1) is the last priority, not the first.

**How to use this on a call:** Don't show all five points. Show the one that matters for that practice. If she says "we're busy but not growing," it's CR7. If she says "patients come in for consults but don't convert," it's CR4. Diagnose the biggest leak, then show the dollar number for fixing it.
